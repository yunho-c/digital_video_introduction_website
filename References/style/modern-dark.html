The Math of Raw Video

Imagine a standard 4K movie. It has 8 million pixels per frame, 3 colors per pixel (Red, Green, Blue), and runs at 60 frames per second.

Resolution 3840 x 2160 (8.3M pixels)
Color Depth 24 bits (3 bytes)
Frame Rate 60 fps
Data Rate ~1.5 GB per second

At that rate, a 2-hour movie would be 10 Terabytes. Yet, on streaming, it's only about 5-10 Gigabytes. How?

Spatial Compression

Intra-frame compression: Shrinking individual images.

This is similar to how JPEGs work. If you have a picture of a blue sky, you don't need to store the color "blue" for every single pixel thousands of times.

Instead, the computer says: "These 500 pixels are all roughly the same shade of blue." This is called Run-Length Encoding or Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) in more complex scenarios. It throws away details your eye can't see (lossy compression).

  • Groups similar pixels into blocks (Macroblocks)
  • Reduces color detail (Chroma Subsampling) because eyes are more sensitive to brightness than color.

Step 2: The Magic

Temporal Compression

Inter-frame compression: Don't repeat yourself.

I

I-Frames (Keyframes)

The full image. Like a standard JPEG. These happen rarely (e.g., once every few seconds) or when the scene changes completely.

P

P-Frames (Predicted)

These only store what changed from the previous frame. If a news anchor sits still and only moves their mouth, P-frames only save the mouth movement.

B

B-Frames (Bi-directional)

The most complex. They look at both the frame before AND the frame after to predict what goes in the middle.

The Translators: Codecs

Compression algorithms are bundled into Codecs (Coder-Decoder). Think of them as different languages for packing the data.

H.264 (AVC)

The Standard

Used everywhere (YouTube, Blu-ray). It's older but highly compatible. Efficient, but struggles with 4K.

H.265 (HEVC)

High Efficiency

Twice as efficient as H.264. Crucial for 4K video. Requires more processing power to decode.

AV1

The Future

Open source and royalty-free. Incredibly efficient (better than H.265) but very slow to encode. Used by Netflix and YouTube for new content.
